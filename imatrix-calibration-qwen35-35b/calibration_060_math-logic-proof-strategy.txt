Here is a beginner-friendly guide to the three most common proof techniques. Think of a proof not as magic, but as a story where every sentence logically follows from the one before it until you reach your final conclusion.

---

## 1. Direct Proof
**"The Straight Line"**

### When to use it:
Use this when the statement is in the form **"If $P$, then $Q$"**. You start with what is true ($P$), use logical steps, and arrive at the result ($Q$) naturally.

### The Strategy:
1.  **Assume** the premise ($P$) is true.
2.  **Show** how your assumptions connect to known facts or definitions.
3.  **Algebraic Manipulation:** Rewrite expressions until you reach the conclusion ($Q$).
4.  **Conclude:** "Since $Q$ followed from $P$, the statement is true."

### The Analogy:
It’s like building a bridge. You stand on the left bank (Assumption) and lay planks step-by-step until you touch the right bank (Conclusion). No teleporting or detours allowed.

### Example: **The sum of two even numbers is even.**

**Goal:** Prove that if $a$ and $b$ are even, then $a + b$ is even.

1.  **Assumption:** Let $a$ be an even number and let $b$ be an even number.
2.  **Definition:** By definition, any even number can be written as "2 times some integer." So:
    *   $a = 2k$ (where $k$ is an integer)
    *   $b = 2m$ (where $m$ is an integer)
3.  **Addition:** Let's add them together:
    *   $a + b = 2k + 2m$
4.  **Factoring:** Pull out the common factor of 2:
    *   $a + b = 2(k + m)$
5.  **Conclusion:** Since $k$ and $m$ are integers, their sum $(k+m)$ is also an integer (let's call it $N$).
    *   Therefore, $a + b = 2N$.
    *   Because the sum looks like "2 times an integer," by definition, **the sum is even.**

## 2. Proof by Contradiction
**"The Reductio ad Absurdum"**

### When to use it:
Use this when a direct path seems impossible or hard to find. It works well for statements that say "X is impossible" or "X does not exist."

### The Strategy:
1.  **Assume the Opposite:** Pretend the thing you are trying to prove is **FALSE**.
2.  **Reasoning:** Proceed with logical steps as if this opposite assumption is true.
3.  **Find a Contradiction:** Eventually, you will hit a wall where something impossible happens (e.g., a number is both even and odd, or $1 = 0$).
4.  **Conclude:** Since the assumption led to nonsense, the assumption must be false. Therefore, your original statement must be true.

### The Analogy:
Imagine you are sure your friend isn't in the house. To prove it, you assume they *are* inside. You check every room, but the doors were locked from the outside, and no windows were opened. Finding that contradiction proves they aren't in the house.

### Example: **The square root of 2 ($\sqrt{2}$) is irrational.**
*(Note: An irrational number cannot be written as a fraction $a/b$).*

1.  **Assumption (The Opposite):** Assume $\sqrt{2}$ is **rational**. This means we can write it as a fraction $\frac{a}{b}$, where $a$ and $b$ are whole numbers with no common factors (it's in "lowest terms").
    *   So, $\sqrt{2} = \frac{a}{b}$.
2.  **Algebra:** Square both sides:
    *   $2 = \frac{a^2}{b^2}$
    *   Multiply by $b^2$: $2b^2 = a^2$
3.  **Logic Step 1 (Evenness):** Look at $a^2$. Since it equals $2 \times b^2$, $a^2$ is clearly **even** (because it has a factor of 2).
4.  **Logic Step 2:** If the square of a number ($a^2$) is even, then the number itself ($a$) must be even. So, we can write $a = 2k$.
5.  **Substitute:** Plug $2k$ back into our equation $2b^2 = a^2$:
    *   $2b^2 = (2k)^2$
    *   $2b^2 = 4k^2$
    *   Divide by 2: $b^2 = 2k^2$
6.  **Logic Step 3:** Look at $b^2$. It equals $2 \times k^2$, so $b^2$ is also **even**. This implies $b$ must be even.
7.  **The Contradiction:**
    *   In Step 1, we assumed $\frac{a}{b}$ had no common factors (lowest terms).
    *   But now we proved both $a$ and $b$ are even (meaning they share a factor of 2).
    *   This is a **contradiction**.
8.  **Conclusion:** Our assumption that $\sqrt{2}$ is rational must be false. Therefore, **$\sqrt{2}$ is irrational.**

## 3. Proof by Mathematical Induction
**"The Domino Effect"**

### When to use it:
Use this when you need to prove a statement is true for **all whole numbers** ($1, 2, 3, \dots$). You usually see "Prove that for all $n \geq 1$..."

### The Strategy:
You don't check every number (there are infinite!). Instead, you rely on two steps:
1.  **Base Case:** Prove it is true for the first number ($n = 1$).
2.  **Inductive Step:** Prove that *if* it works for an arbitrary number $k$, it *must* work for the next number $(k+1)$.
3.  **Conclusion:** If the first domino falls, and every domino knocks over the next one, they all fall.

### The Analogy:
Imagine a long line of dominoes falling. You don't need to push every single one. You just need to ensure:
1.  The first one falls.
2.  Whenever one falls, it is guaranteed to knock over its neighbor.

### Example: **The sum of the first $n$ integers.**
**Goal:** Prove that $1 + 2 + 3 + \dots + n = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}$.

**Step 1: Base Case ($n=1$)**
*   LHS (Left Hand Side): The sum is just **1**.
*   RHS (Right Hand Side): Plug $n=1$ into the formula $\frac{1(1+1)}{2} = \frac{2}{2} = 1$.
*   Since LHS = RHS, the statement is true for $n=1$.

**Step 2: Inductive Step**
*   **Inductive Hypothesis:** Assume the formula is true for some number $k$. (We don't know if $k$ is 5 or 100 yet, just assume it works).
    *   Assumption: $1 + 2 + \dots + k = \frac{k(k+1)}{2}$
*   **Goal:** We must show the formula holds for the *next* number, $(k+1)$.
    *   Target to prove: $1 + 2 + \dots + k + (k+1) = \frac{(k+1)(k+2)}{2}$
*   **Proof:**
    *   Start with the sum up to $k+1$: $(1 + 2 + \dots + k) + (k+1)$
    *   Substitute our Assumption for the part in brackets: $\left[ \frac{k(k+1)}{2} \right] + (k+1)$
    *   Factor out $(k+1)$ from both terms: $(k+1) \left[ \frac{k}{2} + 1 \right]$
    *   Find a common denominator inside the bracket: $(k+1) \left[ \frac{k + 2}{2} \right]$
    *   Rearrange: $\frac{(k+1)(k+2)}{2}$
*   **Result:** This matches our Target exactly.

**Step 3: Conclusion**
Since it works for the start ($n=1$) and working for $k$ guarantees working for $k+1$, the formula is true for **all integers $n \geq 1$**.

### Summary Cheat Sheet

| Method | Think of it as... | Key Question to Ask Yourself |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Direct** | Building a bridge. | "If I start here, can I logically arrive there?" |
| **Contradiction** | Catching a liar. | "What if this were *false*? Would that break reality?" |
| **Induction** | Dominoes falling. | "Does the first one fall, and does one knock over the next?" |

**Tip:** If you get stuck in an Induction proof, look at your Algebra. Often, you have to use "tricks" like factoring or finding common denominators to make the $(k+1)$ side match the formula. That is normal!
