**Setting:** *A steel folding table in the center of an empty city plaza. Blueprints are taped to it, along with a half-eaten pizza box and a set of colorful fabric swatches. DAVID, mid-40s, wearing a hard hat under his arm and reading glasses on a chain, is squinting at calculations. ELENA, late 20s, covered in charcoal smudges, is staring dreamily at the skyline.*

**DAVID:** (Tapping a pen against the table) We have a problem with this one. The "Breath" panels. Specifically, panel four through six. They’re cantilevered three meters out. That’s a lot of leverage for a gust of wind coming off the river.

**ELENA:** It’s not leverage, David. It’s respiration. If they don’t move with the wind, it’s just static metal. I want them to sway *with* the city. Like reeds in a stream.

**DAVID:** Reeds snap when the water gets too fast, Elena. And people have legs. A steel panel swinging three meters out? That’s not "swaying." That's a hazard waiting to be litigated. I’ve calculated the shear stress. At forty miles per hour, the torque exceeds the yield point of this alloy.

**ELENA:** (Laughs softly) It will never get forty miles per hour at street level, my friend. And if it does? Good. People should feel the power of the wind. That’s the point. This isn't a park bench. It's a reaction to the weather.

**DAVID:** The liability insurance is already screaming into their headset. If that "respiration" slaps someone with a shoulder, we are looking at a lawsuit and a permanent shutdown of this plaza. I need safety ratings, Elena. I need rigid points. I need boring, predictable angles.

**ELENA:** (Scooting closer to the table) You're thinking in straight lines. But look at the light coming through there. See how it hits the pavement? That's what I'm capturing. If the panels don't move, the shadows are dead. I want them alive. I want people to walk underneath and feel like they're walking inside a living thing.

**DAVID:** (Takes off his glasses, rubbing his bridge) I know *what* you want. I just worry about *how* we get there without breaking the law or killing someone. It's not about straight lines, Elena. It's about physics. The wind doesn't stop because you say so.

**ELENA:** Okay, okay. (She traces a line on the blueprint) If I make them heavier? No, that makes the sway slower and less graceful. What if we put sensors in? Not mechanical weights, but gyroscopic stabilizers?

**DAVID:** (Raises an eyebrow) Active dampening systems? That changes the budget entirely. And it requires maintenance. Who is changing the batteries on a wind-driven sculpture when it's buried under snow in December?

**ELENA:** (Grins) Who says they’re powered? What if the movement is passive, but controlled by hidden tension cables? Like a suspension bridge?

**DAVID:** (Pauses, looks at the drawing again) Tension rods… internal to the supports. (He grabs his calculator) If I run the numbers on high-tensile wire instead of standard steel bolts… I could reduce the rigidity. Allow for up to fifteen degrees of motion. But only if I reinforce the base foundation. That means pouring more concrete here and here.

**ELENA:** Can you hide the extra weight?

**DAVID:** (Sighs) Maybe under the benches. If we make the benches part of the sculpture's plinth, nobody sees the mass required to hold it up. It costs an extra two grand in materials and two weeks in pouring time.

**ELENA:** Worth it. Can we get the sensors on the light strips? The ones that react when you walk by?

**DAVID:** (Checks the wiring diagram) LEDs are low voltage, sure. But they generate heat. If we encase them in that resin casing you want… ventilation becomes an issue. Overheating will kill the diodes. And humidity from the river could corrode the contacts.

**ELENA:** So seal it?

**DAVID:** We can't just "seal" art, Elena. It needs to breathe, too. That's why we need stainless steel mesh over the lights. For filtration and protection. But that will diffuse the color. The red won't be as punchy.

**ELENA:** (Hesitates) Less punchy… but softer. Like a sunset. Not a traffic light. Okay. I like "soft."

**DAVID:** If it’s soft, we can't use acrylic. Needs to be polycarbonate. Impact resistant. It's heavier. More load again. Elena, this keeps growing. We are fighting gravity with every single decision you make.

**ELENA:** (Smiles) Because gravity is boring! But… I trust you on the concrete and the mesh. I trust that if we get your rigging right, it won't fall down. Just don't put any screws in there visible to the public. No nuts or bolts. They ruin the illusion of floating.

**DAVID:** (Chuckles) You’re asking for custom-molded invisible fasteners. That costs extra. And takes time to machine.

**ELENA:** The installation is on a weekend. We have time. Just make it smooth, David. Let me handle the dream. You just keep the sky from falling on their heads.

**DAVID:** (Looks at her, then back at the blueprints) That’s my actual job description, you know? To keep the sky safe. (He pushes his glasses up) But… fifteen degrees of sway with tension rods? And the polycarbonate diffusers?

**ELENA:** Yes.

**DAVID:** (Nods slowly) It’ll work. If I double the base weight on the plinth. It might shift the center of gravity on the pavement, but it’ll hold. But… I want to see a mockup of that "Breath" panel before we order the steel.

**ELENA:** Deal. We can set one up in your office? Just for twenty-four hours. If you think it looks creepy instead of calming, I'll change the light sequence.

**DAVID:** Fine. But if it makes me dizzy, you're buying lunch.

**ELENA:** (Reaches across and taps his pen) Deal. We're going to make the city stop and look, David. You just do your best to keep us all from getting arrested for negligence while I make that happen.

**DAVID:** (Smiles) That's the plan. Let's finish the math on the foundation.

*(They both lean over the table. The tension dissolves into the sound of pens scratching on paper.)*
