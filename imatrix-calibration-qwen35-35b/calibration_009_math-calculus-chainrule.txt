To find the derivative of the function $f(x) = \sin(x^2) \cdot e^{3x}$, we need to use a combination of three differentiation rules:

1.  **The Product Rule** (because the function is a product of two terms).
2.  **The Chain Rule** (because each term contains an inner function inside another function).
3.  **Basic Trigonometric and Exponential Derivatives.**

Let's break it down step-by-step.

### Step 1: Identify $u$ and $v$ for the Product Rule
The Product Rule states that for a function $f(x) = u \cdot v$:
$$f'(x) = u'v + uv'$$

Here, we define our two terms:
*   $u = \sin(x^2)$
*   $v = e^{3x}$

### Step 2: Differentiate $u$ (requires Chain Rule)
To find $u'$, we differentiate $\sin(x^2)$.
The derivative of the outer function $\sin(\dots)$ is $\cos(\dots)$. We must then multiply by the derivative of the inner function $(x^2)$.

*   Outer derivative: $\frac{d}{dx}[\sin(w)] = \cos(w)$
*   Inner derivative: $\frac{d}{dx}[x^2] = 2x$

Using the **Chain Rule**:
$$u' = \cos(x^2) \cdot (2x) = 2x\cos(x^2)$$

### Step 3: Differentiate $v$ (requires Chain Rule)
To find $v'$, we differentiate $e^{3x}$.
The derivative of the outer function $e^{(\dots)}$ is $e^{(\dots)}$. We must then multiply by the derivative of the inner function $(3x)$.

*   Outer derivative: $\frac{d}{dx}[e^w] = e^w$
*   Inner derivative: $\frac{d}{dx}[3x] = 3$

Using the **Chain Rule**:
$$v' = e^{3x} \cdot (3) = 3e^{3x}$$

### Step 4: Apply the Product Rule
Now, substitute $u$, $u'$, $v$, and $v'$ back into the Product Rule formula ($f'(x) = u'v + uv'$):

1.  **Calculate $u'v$:**
    $$(2x\cos(x^2)) \cdot (e^{3x})$$

2.  **Calculate $uv'$:**
    $$(\sin(x^2)) \cdot (3e^{3x})$$

3.  **Add them together:**
    $$f'(x) = 2x\cos(x^2)e^{3x} + 3e^{3x}\sin(x^2)$$

### Final Answer
We can factor out the common term $e^{3x}$ to simplify the expression:

$$f'(x) = e^{3x} \left( 2x\cos(x^2) + 3\sin(x^2) \right)$$
