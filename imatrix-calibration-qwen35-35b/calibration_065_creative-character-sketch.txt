**Name:** Elias Thorne
**Age:** 68
**Occupation:** Horologist / Former Senior Robotics Engineer
**Current Residence:** A converted carriage house in a cobblestoned district of Berlin.

### **Physical Appearance**
Elias is a man composed of sharp angles and soft edges. His posture is rigid from decades of hunching over microscope rigs, yet his shoulders slump gently when he enters a room, as if he carries the weight of time itself. He has silver-white hair, kept in a disciplined short cut, and pale blue eyes that seem to focus at an impossible depth.

He wears wire-rimmed spectacles on a leather chain—a habit from his engineering days mixed with horology aesthetics. His hands are his most distinct feature: long-fingered and steady, stained perpetually with a thin film of dark machine oil that refuses to scrub out entirely. He favors tweed vests with brass buttons over simple shirts, and always wears a single loupe (magnifying glass) clipped to his belt loop like a jeweler's tool.

### **Professional Background**
Before the silence, there was noise. Elias spent thirty years as a lead systems architect at *Aethelgard Dynamics*, working on high-precision industrial automation and exoskeleton prototypes. He dealt in algorithms, servo-motors, binary logic, and the relentless pursuit of efficiency. He built machines that could assemble cars in seconds or move prosthetics with human-like fluidity.

However, around age 50, Elias experienced a "logic fatigue." The code felt abstract; the output lacked soul. The perfection of the machine was cold. During a sabbatical to visit his grandmother in a village outside Prague, he spent an afternoon watching her repair a grandfather clock using nothing but brass files and patience. He realized that while robots move through *time*, clocks are designed to *mark* it.

He retired early, trading his cleanroom safety shoes for a pair of worn leather boots, leaving Silicon Valley behind for the smell of varnish and gear oil.

### **The Workshop: "The Second Hand"**
Elias’s workspace is a sanctuary of controlled chaos. It does not hum with the electricity of a server farm but thrums with the acoustic rhythm of hundreds of ticking mechanisms simultaneously. Dust motes dance in shafts of amber light that pierce through tall, narrow windows.

Shelves are lined with disassembled chassises: brass pendulums, ruby bearings, and wooden escapements. The air smells of lemon oil, old paper, and solder. In one corner sits a single, disused robotic arm on a stand—an artifact from his past career—used now merely as a tool holder for delicate tweezers. It is the only "electric" thing in the room.

### **Personality & Philosophy**
Elias is quiet, often described by customers as "pleasantly distant." He does not speak much about technology anymore. When he does talk of his past work, it is with a detached nostalgia, viewing robots not as marvels but as "clocks made of steel and electricity."

He is obsessive about accuracy. To Elias, a clock that loses three seconds a day is a liar. He believes in the sanctity of the mechanism: if you touch a gear without cleaning it first, you risk corrosion; if you tighten a screw too hard, you create stress fractures. This precision bled into his social life; he struggles with modern ambiguity and "quick fixes," preferring relationships that require slow winding and regular maintenance.

He is not bitter about the world speeding up; rather, he is protective of the space *between* seconds. He treats every clock restoration like an archeological dig, respecting the history embedded in the wear patterns of the brass.

### **Quirks & Habits**
1.  **The Ticking Sleep:** Elias sleeps to the sound of a specific "grandfather" wall mechanism. He claims it regulates his circadian rhythm better than any alarm clock.
2.  **Conversations with Gears:** When frustrated, he is often heard talking softly to a movement he is repairing. *"Not so fast, old friend. The spring has tension."*
3.  **The Touch:** When meeting people or objects, he instinctively tests the weight and balance of things as if checking for a center of gravity or an imbalance.
4.  **Caffeine Intake:** He drinks Earl Grey tea with no milk, served in heavy porcelain cups that feel substantial in his hands—something he admits feels "properly grounded" compared to disposable mugs.

### **Motivation**
Elias does not restore clocks for money; the prices are often below what his engineering rate would have commanded. He restores them because he is obsessed with *continuity*. He believes that in a world of disposable tech, keeping the past alive through mechanical function is an act of defiance. Every gear that turns is a small victory against entropy.

He hopes one day to build his own masterpiece: a clock that doesn't just tell time, but calculates the exact trajectory of the sun based on the coordinates of his original robotics days—a fusion of his past and present life, where silicon meets steel.
