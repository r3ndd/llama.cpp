The jeep’s engine died, leaving a vacuum of sound that only high altitudes possess. There is no hum here, no distant traffic, only the heavy, settled silence of sleeping stone. I stepped out onto the gravel, and the air hit me immediately—a physical weight, sharp and crystalline, smelling of frost-dampened pine and ancient slate.

It was still the hour of the wolf. The world was painted in monochromes: the deepest indigo of the sky fading slowly into a bruised grey above the jagged peaks that ringed the valley like a defensive fortress. Shadows were long and deceptive, turning the narrow, cobbled lane into a series of dark pools and bright ridges. My breath plumed before me, a ghostly white ribbon that vanished almost instantly, swallowed by the cooling mist that clung to the ground like spun sugar.

I walked toward the village center without a sound. The buildings were huddled together for warmth, their slate roofs slick with condensation, looking like fur-covered beasts crouching under the cold. Yet, they were not dead asleep. From a single chimney on the right, a thin column of charcoal smoke unspooled upward, straight and defiant against the windless air. It was the village’s first breath.

Then came the scent. Woodsmoke. Not the choking kind from a city chimney, but something sweeter, earthier—wood burned slow in a stone hearth, cooking breakfast for someone who has already risen to milk the goats or light the fire. A wooden door creaked open down the street, releasing a sliver of butter-yellow light into the indigo gloom. Inside, the soft clatter of metal on ceramic traveled out into the night before being swallowed by the stone walls.

I stopped at the edge of the main square to wait. The sky was beginning to lose its hardness; the blackness above the highest ridge softened, turning to a deep violet. Somewhere, a rooster crowed—a single, dry sound that didn't announce the morning so much as invite it. It wasn't loud, just necessary.

In that suspended moment, before the sun had even dared to crest the horizon, I felt the distinct privilege of the unobserved. The village was still theirs alone, wrapped in its cold shroud, waiting for the light to turn the mist gold and wake the world. I stood still, breathing the sharp air, letting the blue hour seep into my bones.
