Here is an explanation of the causes of ocean acidification and its specific impacts on shell-forming organisms and marine food webs.

### 1. What Causes Ocean Acidification?

Ocean acidification is primarily caused by the absorption of excess carbon dioxide ($CO_2$) from the atmosphere into the ocean water. The process follows a specific chemical chain reaction:

1.  **Source:** Human activities (burning fossil fuels, deforestation, cement production) release massive amounts of $CO_2$ into the air.
2.  **Absorption:** The ocean acts as a carbon sink, absorbing approximately 30% of the atmospheric $CO_2$.
3.  **Chemical Reaction:** When $CO_2$ dissolves in seawater ($H_2O$), it forms a weak acid called **carbonic acid** ($H_2CO_3$).
4.  **The Shift in pH:** The carbonic acid quickly breaks down into two parts: hydrogen ions ($H^+$) and bicarbonate ions ($HCO_3^-$). An increase in hydrogen ions makes the water more acidic (lowering its pH).
5.  **The Carbonate Scarcity:** This is the most critical step for marine life. The excess hydrogen ions react with existing **carbonate ions** ($CO_3^{2-}$) to create more bicarbonate. By reacting with carbonate, the hydrogen ions effectively "lock up" or remove these essential building blocks from the water.

### 2. Impact on Shell-Forming Organisms

Organisms that rely on calcium carbonate ($CaCO_3$) to build their shells and skeletons are the most immediately threatened by this chemical change. These include corals, oysters, clams, mussels, sea urchins, snails, and microscopic plankton like pteropods (sea butterflies).

The effects occur in two main ways:

*   **Reduced Calcification (Building Shells):**
    Because ocean acidification depletes the concentration of carbonate ions in the water, calcifying organisms must work significantly harder to gather enough material to build and maintain their shells. This requires more energy from the organism. Since energy is a finite resource, the organism has less energy for growth, reproduction, and immune defense.
*   **Dissolution (Shells Dissolving):**
    Under certain conditions of high acidity (low pH) and low saturation state, seawater becomes corrosive to calcium carbonate. This causes shells to dissolve. The microscopic shell-formers known as pteropods are especially vulnerable because they build their shells from *aragonite*, a form of calcium carbonate that is highly susceptible to dissolution compared to other forms like calcite.

### 3. Impact on Marine Food Webs

The effects ripple through the entire food web, starting from the microscopic base and moving up to apex predators.

*   **Collapse at the Base (Plankton):**
    Microscopic plankton are the foundation of the marine food web. Coccolithophores (microscopic algae) and pteropods are essential food sources for salmon, whales, and herring. If these species struggle to survive or grow shells due to acidification, the "food" available for the rest of the ocean diminishes, threatening fish stocks and whale populations.
*   **Destruction of Habitats (Coral Reefs):**
    Coral reefs are often called the "rainforests of the sea," hosting roughly 25% of all marine species. Acidification weakens coral skeletons, making them more brittle and slower to grow. If corals cannot keep up with erosion or storms, entire reef structures collapse. This destroys the shelter and nursery grounds for thousands of fish species.
*   **Physiological Changes in Fish:**
    Recent research shows that ocean acidification affects fish physiology beyond just shell-builders. High acidity can interfere with a fish's blood chemistry (acid-base balance). Studies have shown that larvae exposed to acidic waters often suffer from:
    *   Impaired hearing and smell (making them unable to find reefs or predators).
    *   Loss of the ability to distinguish between predator scents and safe water.
    *   Reduced survival rates in early life stages.

### Summary
In short, ocean acidification is driven by **$CO_2$ emissions** that alter seawater chemistry. It creates an environment where **shell-forming organisms cannot build their homes** or lose energy doing so. This threatens the microscopic base of the food web and destroys coral habitats, leading to a potential **collapse of fisheries** and biodiversity in the oceans.
