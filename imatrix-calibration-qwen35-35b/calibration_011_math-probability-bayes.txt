The estimated post-test probability (Positive Predictive Value) after a positive result is approximately **16.5%**.

Despite the test having high sensitivity and specificity, the low prevalence of the disease means that false positives still vastly outnumber true positives.

Here is the step-by-step breakdown using a hypothetical population of **10,000** people to visualize the numbers:

### 1. The Population Distribution (Prevalence)
*   **Total Population:** 10,000
*   **People with Disease (1%):** 100
*   **People without Disease (99%):** 9,900

### 2. Test Results for Those WITH the Disease (Sensitivity)
*   **True Positives (Positive result, actually has disease):**
    $98\% \text{ of } 100 = \mathbf{98}$ people

### 3. Test Results for Those WITHOUT the Disease (Specificity)
*   *Note: Specificity refers to how well the test identifies healthy people as negative.*
*   **False Positives (Positive result, but is actually healthy):**
    $100\% - 95\% \text{ specificity} = 5\% \text{ error rate}$
    $5\% \text{ of } 9,900 = \mathbf{495}$ people

### 4. Calculate the Total Positive Results
*   $\text{True Positives} + \text{False Positives}$
*   $98 + 495 = \mathbf{593}$ total positive test results

### 5. Calculate Post-Test Probability (PPV)
This asks: "Given that you are one of these 593 people who tested positive, what is the chance you actually have the disease?"

*   $\text{Probability} = \frac{\text{True Positives}}{\text{Total Positives}}$
*   $98 / 593 \approx 0.1652$

### Result
There is a **16.5%** probability that you actually have the disease if you test positive. This phenomenon is known as the *base rate fallacy*, where low disease prevalence causes false positives to overwhelm true positives, even with a highly accurate test.
